UNIT 1: Introduction to Construction

Evolution to building environment

Environment = surroundings which can be natural, man-made or a combination of these.

Built Environment = created by man with or without the aid of the natural environment.

EVOLUTION OF BUILT ENVIRONMENT

  • 1) Pre-Historic dwellings: When man was a hunter & gatherer, dwellings were natural caves. With population increase, man carved more caves + constructed simple structures.
  • 2) Traditional dwellings: Development from simple shelters as population increased + industrialization adapted.
  • 3) Modern buildings: Use functional structural components i.e columns, beams, canopies and reinforced concrete slabs.

Stages involved in building progress

  • 1) Inception: proper briefing of the concerned parties
  • 2) Conception: communication among the designed teams
  • 3) Select: identification of the place where the building has to be constructed
  • 4) Construction: actual doing of the work to come up with the required structure

UNIT 2: Site Investigation

Site investigation - activities that have to be undertaken in order to collect data systematically and in order needed in design and construction process.

Data needed during site investigation

  • a) Site access i.e Roads, boundary lines, location
  • b) Existing services i.e sewer line, water drains, electricity, telephone
  • c) Adjoining structures i.e existing buildings, trees on the adjacent site
  • d) Soil conditions - soil types, properties and ground water condition

Soil Investigation vs Site Investigation

Site Investigation - all embracing term covering every aspect of the site.

Soil Investigation - is related specifically to the sub soil beneath the site.

Purpose of Soil Investigation

  • 1. To determine whether the site is suitable for the proposed project
  • 2. To determine adequate and economic foundation design
  • 3. To determine difficulties which are likely to be encountered during construction
  • 4. To determine the occurrence or cause of changes in sub soil conditions

Soil Samples

Disturbed Soil Samples - obtained from boreholes and trial pits. The structure of the sample is disturbed but are suitable for grading, moisture content and other laboratory test.

Undisturbed Soil Samples - obtained using boring tools in such a manner as to preserve the natural structure. Suitable for rock and clay. Samples are laid in wooden boxes and taken for testing in the laboratory.

Methods of soil investigation

  • 1. Trial pits: Foundations up to 3,000mm deep
  • 2. Borings: Foundations up to 30,000mm deep
  • 3. Deep borings: Foundations over 30,000mm deep

Activities done during site clearance

  • I. Clearance of trees and bushes
  • II. Removal of vegetable soil/top soil
  • III. Removal of termite nets
  • IV. Demolition of unwanted structures

Demolition

I. Taking down

Is the careful removal of all salvageable materials prior to complete destruction. It requires knowledge of load bearing members. May require propping, strutting and shoring.

II. Demolition

Is the complete removal of structural members. A survey of the structure and the neighboring structures must be carried out. Photographs must be taken and stored safely.

Methods of demolition

  • Hand demolition: Using hand tools. NB: Objects should not freely drop if above ground.
  • Pusher arm: Machine with steel pusher arm. Needs firm base with 6 meters clear operating base.
  • Deliberate demolition: Removal of key member causing the structure to collapse. Expert advice required.
  • Ball technique: Swinging demolition ball by a crane against the structure.

UNIT 3: Levelling

LEVELLING - Is process of establishing heights, dimension relative to fixed point of data.

Tools/ equipment used

  • I. Building line/string, II. Hammer/mallet, III. Pegs, IV. Jembes, V. Spade, VI. Graders, bulldozers, rollers

Methods of levelling

I. Cut and fill: Amount of cut = amount of fill. Soil from upper part is deposited on lower part.

II. Cut: Gives undisturbed ground. Disadvantage: cost of removing soil.

III. Fill: Not the best. Risk of settlement, needs deeper foundation.

UNIT 4: Setting Out

SETTING OUT - Is the establishment of marks and lines to determine position and level of construction elements.

First tasks

  • I. Baseline - All setting out relates to it. Coincides with the building line given by the local authority.
  • II. Site level/bench mark - Fixed point like drain cover or post for floor/drainage levels.

3-4-5 Method Sketch

Right angle triangle 3m-4m-5m. Check diagonals AC = BD

SETTING OUT METHODS

I. 3-4-5 Method: Uses Pythagoras theorem. Triangle 3m, 4m, 5m with right angle. Check by diagonals AC & DB must be equal. Advantages: Simple, no calculation, accuracy self-evident.

II. Builders Square Method: Place square at A, extend string = AD. Repeat at B. Check diagonals AC & DB. Advantages: Very accurate for small rectangular buildings.

III. Surveying Instruments: Tripod + sighted. Range limited to 33m.

Profile Boards Sketch

Posts 450-600mm deep, 600-1000mm above. 3 strings show wall thickness + trench width

PROFILE BOARDS

Is the temporary timber framework provided to guide the excavation of the trench. The vertical posts are sunk 450-600mm deep and left 600-1000mm above the ground. The number of nails outline the thickness of the wall and the width of the trench.

NB: Profile boards must be set up before excavation begins.

Setting out a Circular Building Sketch

Peg at centre, string tight, rotate to mark circumference + wall thickness

1. String and spike method: Peg at centre, string tight, move around.

2. Trammel method: Main advantage - not prone to inaccuracy of un-tightening which string and spike has as a disadvantage.

Excavation and Timbering

Excavation

Excavation - is the removal of earth to form a cavity in the ground for foundation or basements.

Trench excavation

Narrow excavation primarily for strip foundation. Can be done manually or mechanically. Can have battered faces or vertically straight faces.

  • Battered faces: Advantage - no temporary supports required. Disadvantage - extra cost of excavation + extra backfilling.
  • Straight faces: Advantage - minimum soil removed = minimum backfilling. Disadvantage - site requires timbering.

Timbering

Timbering - Is the temporary supports provided on the sides of trench to avoid collapsing.

NB: Amount of support required depends on the strength of the soil.

Reasons for timbering

  • I. To protect the operatives working in the excavation
  • II. To keep excavation open by acting as retaining wall to sides of trench

Factors to consider when providing Timbering

  • I. Type of soil
  • II. Time excavation will remain open
  • III. Depth of the excavation
  • IV. Load around the excavation
  • V. Vibration from nearby equipment
  • VI. Weather conditions - rain season requires more support

NB: Timbering provided differently depending on soil type:

  • I. Timbering in hard dry soils
  • II. Timbering in dry loose soils
  • III. Timbering in loose wet soils
  • IV. Timbering in firm soils

Methods of protecting sides of trenches

  • a) Soils heaps - soil heaped along given height to prevent accident
  • b) Side rail - rails erected along trench site on both sides to prevent people from moving close to trench
  • c) Drum filled with soils and having rail/ropes

UNIT 5: Foundations

FOUNDATION

Foundation - is the lower portion of the building usually located below ground level, which transmits the load of the super structure to the sub soil.

Or - part of building in direct contact with ground that transverse all loads to ground safely.

It has to support and safely transmit total load. Foundation is designed to overcome problems like cracking, settling, shrinkage and explosion of both soil and foundation material.

Total Loading comprises:

  • Live loads - movable loads: furniture, human beings
  • Dead loads - fixed loads: walls, roof, floors

Building code requirement {functional requirement}

Building regulation require that:

  • a) Foundation shall safely sustain + transmit combined dead + live loads so as not to cause settlement/movement
  • b) Be of such depth + constructed to avoid damage by swelling or shrinkage of subsoil
  • c) Be capable of resisting chemicals attack by sulphates or other corrosive materials

Functions of foundation

a) Distribution of load: Soil cannot carry load intensity as much as walls/columns. Foundation distributes load over large area so soil takes intensity safely.

b) Minimizing unequal settlement: All walls/columns distribute load for equal settlement. Foundation minimizes unwanted additional forces + unequal settlement.

c) Providing stability: Building should not move/overturn under lateral forces. Foundation provides firm anchorage + stability.

d) Provide level surface: Foundation provides level surface to build plinth + superstructure.

e) Safety against undermining: Foundation provides safety against undermining by floods or burrowing by animals.

f) Prevention of soil movement: Prevents movement of soil under building due to water flow or soil expansion.

Factors to consider in selection of foundation

  • a) Stability - ability to carry loadings without undue settlement/deformation. Depends on type of sub strata + amount of water.
  • b) Loading - ability to support weight + imposed load + transmit forces to natural foundation.
  • c) Ability to sustain small movements at foundation level without endangering load bearing capacity. Achieved by stiff/rigid design or accommodating small movements.

Causes of ground movements

  • a) Subsidence due to underground mining: Tunnels need props. Props corrode/rot → columns collapse → strata above settles.
  • b) Overloading of soils: Deforms sub soils in region of foundation.
  • c) Lowering of groundwater table: Voids previously occupied by water become empty.
  • d) Increasing in water table: Certain soils lose cohesion → load bearing capacity drops.
  • e) Freezing of moisture: Water expands when freezing → frost heave as soil particles forced apart.
  • f) Settlement: If building weight > weight of soil previously on area → settlement expected but acceptable if uniform.

SETTLEMENT

Types of settlement:

  • a) Immediate settlement: Takes place as building is constructed. Occurs in non-cohesive soils.
  • b) Consolidated settlement: Occurs gradually with time, may continue for years after completion. Mainly in cohesive soils i.e clay.

Causes of settlement: a) Deformation of soil due to imposed loads b) Seasonal swelling and shrinkage c) Mass movement d) Vibrations due to pile driving e) Adjacent excavation f) Earth-quakes

Effects of settlement: a) Cracks b) Sinking c) Collapsing d) Sliding e) Overstressing

TYPES OF FOUNDATION

Foundations classified into 2 categories: a) Shallow foundations b) Deep foundations

Shallow foundations: Usually located no more than 2000mm below lowest finished floor. Used when soil close to surface has sufficient bearing capacity + underlying strata do not cause undue settlement.

They include: Strip foundation, Pad foundation, Raft foundation

Deep foundations: Pile foundation, Pier foundation

I. STRIP FOUNDATIONS

a) Shallow/traditional strip foundations: Used where soil has good bearing capacity. Continuous strip of plain concrete spreads load uniformly from walls to subsoil. Spread depends on bearing capacity + shear strength.

Strip Foundation Sketch

Load spreads at 45°. Width = 3x wall. 150mm hardcore base

NB: In all cases W must give adequate working place 450-600mm minimum depending on depth.

Design consideration

  • Load transmitted at 45° from base of wall to soil
  • Depth of strip must be equal to or greater than overall width of wall
  • Width of foundation must be 3 times width of supported wall
  • Compacted hardcore base minimum 150mm for subfloor platform

b) Reinforced / wide strip: Used in subsoil with poor/low bearing capacity: soft sand, clays, silt, build-up soils. Reinforced in transverse + longitudinal direction to resist cracking. Reinforcements lapped at corners.

c) Deep Strip Foundation: Depth 900mm. Used in shrinkable clayey soil to counteract seasonal variation. Clay shrinks dry season, swells wet season. Provides greater resistance to fracture from unequal settlement.

d) Stepped strip foundations: Used on sloping grounds to reduce excavation, backfilling, surplus soil removal, trench timbering. Each step 150mm-225mm high. Lap of concrete at steps not less than concrete foundation depth & not less than 300mm. DPC may also be stepped.

Stepped Strip Foundation Sketch

Each step 150-225mm high. Lap not less than 300mm

2. RAFT FOUNDATION {FLOATING FOUNDATION}

Large continuous footing supporting all columns. Covers entire building area + may extend beyond. Up to 300mm thick reinforced concrete.

Used for: Soils with poor bearing capacity, variations in soil conditions, heavier loads, significant differential settlement.

Types of raft foundations: Unreinforced solid - slab raft, Reinforced solid raft, Beam & slab raft, Cellular raft foundation

Raft Foundation Sketch

Covers entire building area. Supports all columns

a) Solid slab raft foundation: Used when pads overlap, weak subsoil too deep for piles, for breaching weak area, small load-bearing wall buildings. Economically up to 300mm thick. Downstand prevents weathering.

Beam & Slab Raft Sketch

Beams take load + spread to slab. For heavier loads

b) Beam and slab raft foundation: Used when slab thickness exceeds 300mm. Designed for heavier loads. Beams take loads + spread to monolithic slab. Suitable where thin layer of stiff soil overlies weak strata.

c) Cellular raft foundation: Used where differential settlement likely + foundation supports considerable load. Where stress in raft too high, rigidity required, beams exceed 900mm deep.

Advantages: Can be used in car park, storage space, accommodate services, supermarkets.
