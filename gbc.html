SETTLEMENT

Types of settlement

  • a) Immediate settlement: Takes place as building is constructed. Occurs in non-cohesive soils.
  • b) Consolidated settlement: Occurs gradually with time, may continue for years after completion. Mainly in cohesive soils i.e clay.

Causes of settlement

  • a) Deformation of soil due to imposed loads
  • b) Seasonal swelling and shrinkage of soils
  • c) Mass movement of ground in unstable areas
  • d) Vibrations due to pile driving
  • e) Adjacent excavation
  • f) Earth-quakes

Effects of settlement on building

  • a) Cracks in the building
  • b) Sinking of the building
  • c) Collapsing of the building
  • d) Sliding of the building
  • e) Overstressing of the building

TYPES OF FOUNDATION

Foundations classified into 2 categories:

  • a) Shallow foundations
  • b) Deep foundations

Shallow foundations

Usually located no more than 2000mm below lowest finished floor. Used when soil close to surface has sufficient bearing capacity + underlying strata do not cause undue settlement.

They include:

a) Strip foundation

  • • Shallow/traditional strip foundation
  • • Reinforced/wide foundation
  • • Deep strip foundation
  • • Stepped strip foundation

b) Pad foundation

  • • Spread/isolated footing
  • • Combined footing
  • • Strap footing

c) Raft foundation

Deep foundations

  • a) Pile foundation
  • b) Pier foundation

I. STRIP FOUNDATIONS

a) Shallow/traditional strip foundations

Used where soil has good bearing capacity. Continuous strip of plain concrete spreads load uniformly from walls to subsoil. Spread depends on bearing capacity + shear strength.

NB: In all cases W must give adequate working place 450-600mm minimum depending on depth.

Design consideration

  • Load transmitted at 45° from base of wall to soil
  • Depth of strip must be equal to or greater than overall width of wall
  • Width of foundation must be 3 times width of supported wall
  • Compacted hardcore base minimum 150mm for subfloor platform

b) Reinforced / wide strip

Used in subsoil with poor/low bearing capacity: soft sand, clays, silt, build-up soils. Reinforced in transverse + longitudinal direction to resist cracking. Reinforcements lapped at corners.

c) Deep Strip Foundation

Depth 900mm. Used in shrinkable clayey soil to counteract seasonal variation. Clay shrinks dry season, swells wet season. Provides greater resistance to fracture from unequal settlement.

d) Stepped strip foundations

Used on sloping grounds to reduce excavation, backfilling, surplus soil removal, trench timbering. Each step 150mm-225mm high. Lap of concrete at steps not less than concrete foundation depth & not less than 300mm. DPC may also be stepped.

2. RAFT FOUNDATION {FLOATING FOUNDATION}

Large continuous footing supporting all columns. Covers entire building area + may extend beyond. Up to 300mm thick reinforced concrete.

Used for: Soils with poor bearing capacity, variations in soil conditions, heavier loads, significant differential settlement.

Types of raft foundations

  • • Unreinforced solid - slab raft
  • • Reinforced solid raft
  • • Beam & slab raft
  • • Cellular raft foundation

a) Solid slab raft foundation

Used when pads overlap, weak subsoil too deep for piles, for breaching weak area, small load-bearing wall buildings. Economically up to 300mm thick. Downstand prevents weathering.

b) Beam and slab raft foundation

Used when slab thickness exceeds 300mm. Designed for heavier loads. Beams take loads + spread to monolithic slab. Suitable where thin layer of stiff soil overlies weak strata.

c) Cellular raft foundation

Used where differential settlement likely + foundation supports considerable load. Where stress in raft too high, rigidity required, beams exceed 900mm deep.

Advantages: Can be used in car park, storage space, accommodate services, supermarkets.
