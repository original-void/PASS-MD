Topic 1: Chain Survey

Basic linear measurements using chain/tape

1. Principles of Surveying Page 1

2 Fundamental Principles:

  1. Work from whole to part: Establish main control points first with high accuracy, then survey detail points from them.
  2. Locate new point by at least 2 measurements: A point should be fixed by minimum 2 independent measurements from fixed reference points.
Why: Working part-to-whole makes small errors accumulate and ruin the whole survey. Whole-to-part contains errors within small areas only.

3. Ranging & Chaining Methods Pages 4-5

Ranging: Establishing intermediate points in a straight line between 2 end stations so chain lies straight.

Direct Ranging: When end stations are intervisible. Done by eye or ranging rod.

Indirect Ranging: When end stations not intervisible due to obstruction. Uses reciprocal ranging.

Chaining: Measuring distance by laying chain along the line.

Procedure for Chaining:

  1. Leader goes ahead with 10 arrows, follower holds zero end
  2. Leader drags chain, aligns with ranging rod at far station
  3. Follower checks zero end is at station peg
  4. Leader fixes arrow at rear end of chain
  5. Both lift chain, swing it, and lay it again
  6. Count arrows = number of full chain lengths
$$\text{Total Distance} = (n \times \text{Chain Length}) + \text{Tail Length}$$

4. Obstacles in Chaining Page 6

Type 1: Obstruction to ranging only - River, bush. Solution: Erect perpendiculars, use right angle triangles.

Type 2: Obstruction to chaining + ranging - Building, pond. Solution: Use triangle method or rectangle method.

Overcoming obstacle by constructing rectangle

5. Errors in Chain Survey Pages 7-8

Compensating Errors: Random errors. Sometimes +ve, sometimes -ve. Cancel out partly. Proportional to √L

Cumulative Errors: Systematic errors. Always +ve or always -ve. Add up. Proportional to L

Cumulative +veCumulative -ve
Chain too shortChain too long
Temperature > standardTemperature < standard
Sagging of chainPull more than standard
$$\text{True Length} = \text{Measured Length} \times \frac{\text{Actual Chain Length}}{\text{Nominal Chain Length}}$$

Topic 2: Compass Survey

Measuring angles using prismatic compass

6. Bearings - WCB & QB Page 9

Whole Circle Bearing WCB: Angle measured clockwise from North 0° to 360°. Always 0°-360°.

Quadrantal Bearing QB: Angle measured from North or South towards East or West. 0°-90° + quadrant name.

8. Local Attraction Pages 11-12

Local Attraction: When compass needle is attracted by magnetic objects like iron pipes, power lines near station.

Detection: FB and BB of same line should differ by 180°. If not, local attraction present.

Correction: Apply correction to affected stations. If only 1 station affected, use unaffected bearing to correct others.

$$\text{Correction} = \text{True Bearing} - \text{Observed Bearing}$$

9. Included Angles from Bearings Page 13

For clockwise traverse:

$$\text{Included Angle} = \text{BB of previous line} - \text{FB of next line}$$

If result -ve, add 360°. If >180°, subtract from 360° for interior angle.

Topic 3: Leveling

Finding heights and differences in elevation

10. Terms in Leveling Page 16

BS - Back Sight:First reading on point of known elevation. +ve
IS - Intermediate Sight:Readings between BS and FS
FS - Fore Sight:Last reading before shifting instrument. -ve
HI - Height of Instrument:RL of line of sight = RL of BM + BS
RL - Reduced Level:Elevation of point above datum
Why: HI stays constant for all readings from one instrument position. Change point = new HI.

11. Height of Instrument Method Pages 17-18

$$\text{HI} = \text{RL of BM} + \text{BS}$$
$$\text{RL of any point} = \text{HI} - \text{Staff Reading}$$
Why: Find instrument height once, then subtract all readings from it. Faster for many points.

Example:

BM RL = 100.000m, BS = 1.250m, IS = 0.980m, FS = 2.150m. Find RLs.

HI = 100.000 + 1.250 = 101.250m

RL of IS = 101.250 - 0.980 = 100.270m

RL of FS = 101.250 - 2.150 = 99.100m

12. Rise & Fall Method Pages 19-20

$$\text{Rise} = \text{Previous Reading} - \text{Current Reading}$$
$$\text{Fall} = \text{Current Reading} - \text{Previous Reading}$$
$$\text{RL of point} = \text{RL of previous point} ± \text{Rise/Fall}$$
Why: Each RL calculated step by step. Better for checking errors as you go.

13. Arithmetic Checks Page 21

For HI Method:

$$\sum \text{BS} - \sum \text{FS} = \text{Last RL} - \text{First RL}$$

For Rise & Fall Method:

$$\sum \text{BS} - \sum \text{FS} = \sum \text{Rise} - \sum \text{Fall} = \text{Last RL} - \text{First RL}$$
Why: Checks if addition of staff readings and RL calculations are correct. Must balance.

14. Contouring Pages 22-24

Contour: Line joining points of equal elevation above datum.

Contour Interval CI: Vertical distance between 2 consecutive contours.

Horizontal Equivalent HE: Horizontal distance between 2 contours on map.

$$\text{Gradient} = \frac{\text{CI}}{\text{HE}}$$
Why: Steep slope = contours close together. Flat ground = contours far apart.

15. Characteristics of Contours Page 25

  1. Contour lines never cross each other except in case of overhanging cliff.
  2. Contour lines close together = steep slope. Far apart = gentle slope.
  3. Uniform spacing = uniform slope.
  4. Contour lines form V-shape pointing upstream when crossing valley.
  5. Contour lines form U-shape pointing downstream when crossing ridge.
