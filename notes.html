1. Define Survey & Objectives

Define Survey:

Surveying is the art of determining relative positions of the points on earth's surface.

It is done through observation & taking measurements & determining the boundaries, sizes, positions, quantity, value etc of lands, estates, buildings, farms etc.

Objectives of Surveying

Primary purpose of surveying is to measure and record field data from which accurate plans & maps can be prepared, usually through:

i) Determining horizontal distance between two or more points on the earth's surface

ii) Determining vertical distance between two different points on the earth's surface

iii) Determining the area of a given piece of land

iv) Locating the directions of various features on the earth's surface

2. Classification of Survey

Surveys can be classified based on several factors. The most common classification is based on purpose & field application:

- By instrument used

- By method employed

3. Based on Earth's Curvature

This is the primary fundamental classification of survey. It's further divided into two types:

a) Geodetic surveys

These are surveys that cover a large area, i.e., a country or a continent. It uses complex mathematics and a spheroidal model of the earth.

b) Plane survey

These are surveys done in small areas. The surface is assumed to be a flat plane. This simplifies calculations & it is mostly used for engineering projects, cross country highways.

N/B: Unless you are building a tunnel you will always use plane survey.

4. Based on Purpose & Field of Application

Topographic survey: This is survey done to gather data of natural & manmade features on the earth & represent them on a map.

b) Cadastral Survey

This is survey done to determine and mark property boundaries. It is also a legally binding type of survey.

c) Engineering Survey

Done to collect data for the planning, design & construction of engineering works. It involves setting out where dimensions are transferred on the ground with dimension control.

d) Control Survey

Is done to establish a network of horizontal & vertical reference points (bench marks) across a project and provides the fundamental framework of control points.

5. Based on Instrument Used

i) Theodolite survey: Measuring horizontal and vertical angles

ii) Compass / Chain: Measuring direction / bearings

iii) Tapes & Ranging rods: Measuring distances for small and open areas

iv) Dumpy levels, Automatic level & Digital levels: Determine height differences and elevations

v) Echo sounders: Mapping bed & features of water bodies

vi) Drones & Cameras: Creating maps and 3D models from aerial photographs

vii) Total station: Electronic distance measurement using electromagnetic waves

viii) Remote sensing: Satellites gather information without physical interference

ix) GPS survey: Determines absolute position i.e. latitude, longitude using satellite

6. Based on Method Employed

i) Triangulation: System of triangles are established, angles are measured precisely & only a few baseline distances are needed.

ii) Traversing: Series of connected lines are established, the length & directions of this line are measured. Most common for construction sites.

iii) Trilateration: Network of triangles established by measuring all sides with EDM, no angles measured. Uses GPS principle.

7. Principles of Survey

Principle of working from whole to part: First identify primary control points with high precision, then secondary, then tertiary.

Control point: Point on ground whose horizontal and vertical location is known. Primary points located with sophisticated equipment, then secondary, then tertiary.

Using measurements from two known control points: Given A & B with known length and bearing, other points fixed by intersection.

Accuracy: Allows tolerance error. Precision: Demands exactness.

8. Process of Surveying

Done in three main phases:

1. Reconnaissance: Pre-field inspection to understand terrain, choose method, estimate cost/time, select stations

2. Field Work & Measurement: Actual measurement and recording. Surveyor must be familiar with equipment

3. Office Work: Post-field data processing. Prepare charts, plans and maps for client

9. Horizontal Distance Measurement

Basic measurement determining distance between 2 points for fixing positions, setting out & scaling.

1. Pacing: Distance = No. of paces × Avg pace length

2. Tachometry: Indirect using Theodolite. 3. Chaining: Direct with tape/chain. 4. EDM: Using light/electromagnetic waves

10. Errors in Surveying

Error = Measured value - True value

i) Gross errors: Carelessness, misreading, wrong station. Large, irregular, cannot be corrected mathematically

ii) Systematic Errors: Follow definite pattern, same direction. Causes: instrumental, natural, personal

iii) Random Errors: Small unpredictable ± variations. Cancel out with repeated observations

12. Chain Surveying Basics

Simplest method using linear measurements only. Area divided into triangles because triangle can be plotted uniquely if 3 sides known.

Principle: Triangulation

Types of Chain: Gunter 66ft=100 links, Engineer 100ft=100 links, Metric 20m=100 links, 30m=150 links

13. Chain Equipment & Basic Terms

Key Equipment: Chain, Steel tape, Arrows, Ranging rods, Pegs, Offset rod, Cross staff

Basic Terms: Survey Station, Baseline, Check line, Tie line, Offsets, Survey line

Offsets: Lateral measurements from survey line. Perpendicular offset at 90° is most accurate.

14. Procedure for Chaining

1. Reconnaissance 2. Choice of Stations 3. Station Marking 4. Witnessing 5. Offsetting 6. Sketching

N/B: Chain must be pulled tight, horizontal, and free from sag for accurate measurements.

15. Corrections in Chain Surveying

All field measurements subject to errors. Corrections applied for true distances:

1. Standardization: If chain too long: subtract. If too short: add

2. Temperature:

$$C_t = L \times \alpha \times (T_m - T_o)$$

3. Pull/Tension:

$$C_p = \frac{(P - P_o)L}{AE}$$

4. Sag:

$$C_s = \frac{W^2L}{24P^2}$$
Always negative

5. Slope: Reduce to horizontal: $H = L \cos \theta$

16. Levelling Terms

Key Terms: Datum, Reduced Level RL, Bench Mark BM, Back Sight BS, Fore Sight FS, Intermediate Sight IS, Height of Instrument HI, Change Point CP

17. Levelling Methods

1. Rise and Fall Method: Rise = BS - FS if BS > FS. RL next = RL previous + Rise OR - Fall

Check: ΣBS - ΣFS = ΣRise - ΣFall = Last RL - First RL

2. Height of Instrument Method: HI = RL + BS, RL = HI - FS or IS

Check: ΣBS - ΣFS = Last RL - First RL

Reciprocal Levelling: Used across rivers. True difference = (h_A + h_B) / 2

Curvature & Refraction: Combined correction C = -0.0673D² meters where D in km

20. Contours

Contour: Imaginary line joining points of equal elevation

Contour Interval: Vertical distance between consecutive contours

Characteristics: Same elevation, close = steep slope, never cross, V-shape upstream for valleys

Methods of Contouring

1. Direct Method: Points of same elevation located and plotted. Very accurate but slow. Used for detailed engineering works, roads, railways.

Advantages: Very accurate, suitable for small areas

Disadvantages: Slow, expensive, tedious for large areas

2. Indirect Method: Spot levels taken at regular intervals, contours interpolated.

a) Grid Method: Area divided into squares, levels at corners

b) Cross-section Method: Cross-sections taken at regular intervals along centerline

c) Radial Line Method: Radiating lines from central point, levels taken along lines

Advantages: Faster, economical, suitable for large areas

Disadvantages: Less accurate than direct method
