1. Direct Stress & Strain Pages 1-2

Direct Stress

When force is applied on a body, it deforms. To resist deformation, internal resistance is offered by material per unit area. This is called Stress.

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A}$$
Why this formula: Stress = How hard you pull \(F\) ÷ How thick the material is \(A\). Same force on thinner area = higher stress.

Where: \(\sigma\) = Stress, \(F\) = Force, \(A\) = Cross-sectional area

Units: N/m² or Pa. 1 N/mm² = 1 MN/m² = 10⁶ N/m²

Strain

When a bar is subjected to force, it elongates or shortens. Strain is the ratio of change in length to original length.

$$\varepsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L}$$
Why this formula: Strain = How much it stretched ÷ Original length. Strain has no unit because mm ÷ mm cancels.

2. Hooke's Law & Elastic Constants Pages 3-5

Hooke's Law: Within elastic limit, stress is directly proportional to strain.

$$\sigma \propto \varepsilon \quad \text{or} \quad \sigma = E \varepsilon$$
Why this formula: \(E\) = Stiffness of material. Steel has high \(E\), rubber has low \(E\). Stress = Stiffness × How much it stretched.

Modulus of Rigidity (G)

$$G = \frac{\tau}{\phi}$$
Why: For shear/twisting. Shear stress ÷ Shear strain = Rigidity.

Bulk Modulus (K)

$$K = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_v}$$
Why: For pressure from all sides. Direct stress ÷ Volume change = Bulk modulus.

Poisson's Ratio (ν)

$$\nu = -\frac{\text{Lateral Strain}}{\text{Longitudinal Strain}}$$
Why: When you pull a bar, it gets longer but thinner. This ratio tells how much thinner.

3. Factor of Safety Pages 5-6

$$\text{F.O.S} = \frac{\text{Ultimate Stress}}{\text{Working Stress}}$$
Why this formula: We never design at breaking point. FOS = Breaking stress ÷ Actual stress used. If FOS = 3, we use 1/3 of breaking stress.

4. Example: Bar of Uniform Section Page 7

Question:

A bar 1000 mm long and 20 mm × 20 mm in cross-section is subjected to an axial pull of 40 kN. If \(E = 80\) GN/m², calculate the elongation of the bar.

Given: \(L = 1000\) mm, \(b = 20\) mm, \(d = 20\) mm, \(F = 40\) kN = \(40 \times 10^3\) N, \(E = 80\) GN/m² = \(80 \times 10^3\) N/mm²

Solution:

Step 1: Find Area

$$A = b \times d = 20 \times 20 = 400 \text{ mm²}$$
Why: Area resists the force. Bigger area = less stress.

Step 2: Use Hooke’s Law for Elongation

$$\Delta L = \frac{FL}{AE}$$
Why this formula: Force stretches bar. Longer bar stretches more \(L\). Stiffer material \(E\) or bigger area \(A\) reduces stretch.
$$\Delta L = \frac{40 \times 10^3 \times 1000}{400 \times 80 \times 10^3} = 1.250 \text{ mm}$$

Answer: 1.250 mm

5. Example: Hollow Cylinder Page 9

Question:

A hollow cast iron cylinder 4 m long, 300 mm outer dia, 50 mm thick, carries axial compressive load 500 kN. Find stress and contraction. Take \(E = 150\) GPa.

Given: \(L = 4\) m = 4000 mm, \(D = 300\) mm, \(t = 50\) mm, \(F = 500\) kN = \(500 \times 10^3\) N, \(E = 150 \times 10^3\) N/mm²

Solution:

Step 1: Find Inner Dia & Area

Inner dia \(d = D - 2t = 300 - 100 = 200\) mm

$$A = \frac{\pi}{4}(D^2 - d^2) = \frac{\pi}{4}(300^2 - 200^2) = 39269.908 \text{ mm²}$$
Why: For hollow section, subtract inner hole area from outer circle area.

Step 2: Find Stress

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{500 \times 10^3}{39269.908} = 12.732 \text{ N/mm²}$$
Why: Stress = Force per unit area. Same force on smaller area = more stress. Compressive.

Step 3: Find Contraction

$$\Delta L = \frac{FL}{AE} = \frac{500 \times 10^3 \times 4000}{39269.908 \times 150 \times 10^3} = 0.3395 \text{ mm}$$
Why: Same elongation formula, but load is compressive so bar shortens.

Answer: Stress = 12.732 N/mm², Contraction = 0.340 mm

6. Bars of Varying Cross-Section Pages 10-11

For stepped bar with sections \(L_1, A_1\) and \(L_2, A_2\):

$$\Delta L = \frac{PL_1}{A_1E} + \frac{PL_2}{A_2E}$$
Why: Each section stretches by its own amount. Total stretch = sum of both. Thinner section stretches more.

8. Composite Bars Theory Pages 12-14

For composite bar with materials 1 and 2:

$$F_1 = \frac{F A_1 E_1}{A_1 E_1 + A_2 E_2} \quad , \quad F_2 = \frac{F A_2 E_2}{A_1 E_1 + A_2 E_2}$$
Why: Stiffer material takes more load. Load splits based on stiffness \(AE\). Both materials stretch equally.
$$\Delta L = \frac{F_1 L}{A_1 E_1} = \frac{F_2 L}{A_2 E_2}$$

9. Example: R.C Square Column Pages 14-16

Question:

A reinforced concrete column 300 mm × 300 mm has 4 bars of 20 mm dia. If modular ratio \(m = 18\) and max stress in concrete \(\sigma_c = 6\) N/mm², find the safe load the column can carry.

Given: Gross size = 300×300, 4 bars 20 mm dia, \(m = 18\), \(\sigma_c = 6\) N/mm²

R.C Square Column: 300×300mm with 4×20mm dia bars

Solution:

Step 1: Find Steel Area

$$A_s = n \times \frac{\pi}{4}d^2 = 4 \times \frac{\pi}{4} \times 20^2 = 1256.637 \text{ mm²}$$
Why: Total steel = number of bars × area of one bar.

Step 2: Find Concrete Area

$$A_c = \text{Gross Area} - A_s = 300 \times 300 - 1256.637 = 88743.363 \text{ mm²}$$
Why: Concrete area = total column area minus steel area.

Step 3: Stress Relation

$$\sigma_s = m \sigma_c = 18 \times 6 = 108 \text{ N/mm²}$$
Why: Steel is 18x stiffer than concrete. For same strain, steel takes 18x more stress.

Step 4: Load Capacity

$$P = A_c\sigma_c + A_s\sigma_s$$
Why: Total load = load taken by concrete + load taken by steel.
$$P = 88743.363 \times 6 + 1256.637 \times 108 = 668140.680 \text{ N}$$

Answer: 668.141 kN

10. Example: R.C Circular Column Page 17

Question:

A R.C circular column 400 mm dia has 5 bars of 20 mm dia. If \(m = 18\), \(\sigma_c \leq 5\) N/mm² and \(\sigma_s \leq 120\) N/mm², find safe load.

Given: \(D = 400\) mm, 5 bars 20 mm dia, \(m = 18\), \(\sigma_c \leq 5\), \(\sigma_s \leq 120\) N/mm²

R.C Circular Column: 400mm dia with 5×20mm bars

Solution:

Step 1: Check Which Governs

If \(\sigma_c = 5\), then \(\sigma_s = m\sigma_c = 18 \times 5 = 90\) N/mm² < 120 ✓ OK

Why: Concrete reaches max stress first. If steel governed, concrete would be underutilized.

Step 2: Areas

$$A_s = 5 \times \frac{\pi}{4} \times 20^2 = 1570.796 \text{ mm²}$$
$$A_g = \frac{\pi}{4} \times 400^2 = 125663.706 \text{ mm²}$$
$$A_c = 125663.706 - 1570.796 = 124092.910 \text{ mm²}$$

Step 3: Load

$$P = A_c\sigma_c + A_s\sigma_s = 124092.910 \times 5 + 1570.796 \times 90$$
$$P = 761836.190 \text{ N} = 761.836 \text{ kN}$$

Answer: 761.836 kN

11. Thermal Stress Theory Pages 18-19

Free expansion: \(\Delta L = L \alpha t\)

Why: Heat makes things expand. Expansion = Original length × Expansion coefficient × Temp rise.

Thermal strain: \(\varepsilon = \alpha t\)

Why: Strain = Expansion ÷ Original length = \(\alpha t\).

If restrained: \(\sigma = E \alpha t\)

Why: If bar can't expand, strain converts to stress using Hooke's law \(\sigma = E\varepsilon\).

Thermal force: \(F = EA\alpha t\)

Why: Force = Stress × Area. Restraint force needed to stop expansion.

4-Step Method

  1. Calculate free expansion: \(\Delta L = L\alpha t\)
  2. Find force to restore original length
  3. Calculate stress: \(\sigma = E\varepsilon\)
  4. Determine if tensile or compressive

12. Example: Thermal Stress - Fixed Ends Page 20

Question:

A brass rod 20 m long is fixed at both ends. If max stress allowed is 76.5 N/mm², find temperature rise it can withstand. \(\alpha = 17 \times 10^{-6}\) per °C, \(E = 90\) kN/mm².

Given: \(\sigma = 76.5\) N/mm², \(\alpha = 17 \times 10^{-6}\), \(E = 90 \times 10^3\) N/mm²

Solution:

$$\sigma = E\alpha t$$
Why: Fixed ends mean no expansion allowed. All thermal strain \(\alpha t\) becomes stress.
$$t = \frac{\sigma}{E\alpha} = \frac{76.5}{90 \times 10^3 \times 17 \times 10^{-6}} = 50.000°C$$

Answer: 50.000°C rise

13. Example: Thermal Stress - Stepped Bar Page 23

Question:

A stepped steel bar is fixed between rigid supports. Section 1: 40 mm dia, 200 mm long. Section 2: 25 mm dia, 300 mm long. Temperature rises 50°C. Find stresses. \(E = 200\) kN/mm², \(\alpha = 12 \times 10^{-6}\) per °C.

Given: \(d_1 = 40\) mm, \(L_1 = 200\) mm, \(d_2 = 25\) mm, \(L_2 = 300\) mm, \(t = 50\)°C, \(E = 200 \times 10^3\) N/mm², \(\alpha = 12 \times 10^{-6}\)

Fixed supports prevent expansion → compressive stress

Solution:

Step 1: Areas

$$A_1 = \frac{\pi}{4} \times 40^2 = 1256.637 \text{ mm²}$$
$$A_2 = \frac{\pi}{4} \times 25^2 = 490.874 \text{ mm²}$$

Step 2: Free Expansion

$$\Delta L_{free} = (L_1 + L_2)\alpha t = 500 \times 12 \times 10^{-6} \times 50 = 0.300 \text{ mm}$$
Why: If free, whole bar would expand 0.300 mm.

Step 3: Force to Compress Back

Let compressive force = \(F\). Total contraction = 0.300 mm

$$\frac{F L_1}{A_1 E} + \frac{F L_2}{A_2 E} = 0.300$$
Why: Fixed ends mean total contraction from force = free expansion. Solve for F.
$$F = \frac{0.300 \times 200 \times 10^3}{\frac{200}{1256.637} + \frac{300}{490.874}} = 77469.991 \text{ N}$$

Step 4: Stresses

$$\sigma_1 = \frac{F}{A_1} = \frac{77469.991}{1256.637} = 61.647 \text{ N/mm²}$$
$$\sigma_2 = \frac{F}{A_2} = \frac{77469.991}{490.874} = 157.813 \text{ N/mm²}$$

Answer: Section 1 = 61.647 N/mm², Section 2 = 157.813 N/mm² (Both Compressive)

14. Examples: Composite Thermal Stress Pages 22-25

Example 1: Steel + Aluminum Sandwich Page 24

Question:

A steel plate 20×3 mm is sandwiched between two aluminum plates 20×6 mm each. Temperature rises 50°C. Find the stresses in each material.

Given Data:

Steel:\(E_s = 200\) kN/mm²\(\alpha_s = 12 \times 10^{-6}\) per °C
Aluminum:\(E_{al} = 80\) kN/mm²\(\alpha_{al} = 24 \times 10^{-6}\) per °C
Temp rise:\(t = 50\)°C

Areas:

Steel: \(A_s = 20 \times 3 = 60\) mm²

Aluminum: \(A_{al} = 2 \times 20 \times 6 = 240\) mm²   (2 plates)

Logic: Aluminum expands more than steel when heated. Since they’re bolted together, aluminum gets compressed and steel gets pulled in tension.

Step 1: Compatibility

$$\frac{\sigma_s}{E_s} + \alpha_s t = \frac{\sigma_{al}}{E_{al}} + \alpha_{al} t$$

Step 2: Force Balance

$$A_s \sigma_s + A_{al} \sigma_{al} = 0$$
Total tensile force in steel = Total compressive force in aluminum

Step 3: Solve

From force balance: \(60 \sigma_s = -240 \sigma_{al}\) → \(\sigma_s = -4 \sigma_{al}\)

Substituting: \(\sigma_{al} = -32.000\) N/mm², \(\sigma_s = +128.000\) N/mm²

When heated: Aluminum tries to expand 2x more than Steel but is restrained
Answer: Aluminum = -32.000 N/mm² (Compression), Steel = +128.000 N/mm² (Tension)

Example 2: Gun Metal Rod + Steel Tube Page 25

Question:

A gun metal rod 20 mm diameter is inside a steel tube with 25 mm ID and 30 mm OD. They are bolted at the ends. Temperature rises 200°F. Find the stresses.

Given Data:

Steel:\(E_s = 200\) kN/mm²\(\alpha_s = 6 \times 10^{-6}\) per °F
Gun Metal:\(E_{gm} = 100\) kN/mm²\(\alpha_{gm} = 10 \times 10^{-6}\) per °F
Temp rise:\(t = 200\)°F

Areas:

GM Rod: \(A_{gm} = \frac{\pi}{4} \times 20^2 = 314.159\) mm²

Steel Tube: \(A_s = \frac{\pi}{4}(30^2 - 25^2) = 215.985\) mm²

Logic: Gun metal has higher \(\alpha\), so it wants to expand more. Since bolted, GM gets compressed and steel tube gets tension.

Force Balance:

$$A_{gm} \sigma_{gm} + A_s \sigma_s = 0$$

Solving with compatibility:

$$\sigma_{gm} = -72.727 \text{ N/mm²}, \quad \sigma_s = +105.818 \text{ N/mm²}$$
Bolted at ends: GM expands more → compressed, Steel restrains → tension
Answer: GM Rod = -72.727 N/mm² (Compression), Steel Tube = +105.818 N/mm² (Tension)

15. Types of Supports & Beams Page 26

1. Simply Supported Beam: Rests on two supports at ends. Free to rotate but no vertical movement. Reactions: Vertical only at each support.

2. Cantilever Beam: Fixed at one end, free at other. Fixed end has Vertical reaction, Horizontal reaction, and Moment.

3. Overhanging Beam: Simply supported but extends beyond one or both supports. Overhang causes negative BM.

4. Fixed Beam: Both ends fixed. No rotation or translation. 6 unknowns total.

5. Continuous Beam: More than two supports. Statically indeterminate.

Types of beams and supports

16. Types of Loading Page 27

1. Point Load / Concentrated Load: Acts at a single point. Units: N or kN. Example: Person standing on beam.

2. Uniformly Distributed Load (UDL): Constant intensity over length. Units: N/m or kN/m. Total load = w × L. Example: Weight of slab.

3. Uniformly Varying Load (UVL) / Triangular Load: Intensity varies linearly from zero to max. Total load = ½ × w × L. Acts at centroid: L/3 from max end.

4. Couple / Applied Moment: Pure rotation, no resultant force. Units: N·m or kN·m. Causes sudden jump in BMD.

Different types of loads on beams

17. Reaction Calculations - Basic Page 28

Equilibrium Equations:

$$\sum F_y = 0 \quad \text{(Sum of vertical forces = 0)}$$
$$\sum M = 0 \quad \text{(Sum of moments about any point = 0)}$$
$$\sum F_x = 0 \quad \text{(Sum of horizontal forces = 0, usually not needed)}$$
Why: Beam must not move up/down or rotate. These 2 equations solve for 2 unknown reactions.

Example 1: UDL on Simply Supported Beam

Beam 6m long, UDL 2 kN/m over entire span. Find reactions.

Step 1: Total Load

$$W = w \times L = 2 \times 6 = 12 \text{ kN}$$

Step 2: By Symmetry

$$R_A = R_B = \frac{W}{2} = \frac{12}{2} = 6 \text{ kN}$$
Why: Load is symmetric, so reactions are equal. Or use moments: \(\sum M_A = 0\) → \(R_B \times 6 - 12 \times 3 = 0\) → \(R_B = 6\) kN

Example 2: Point Load

Beam 5m, point load 10 kN at 2m from left support. Find reactions.

Taking moments about A:

$$\sum M_A = 0: \quad R_B \times 5 - 10 \times 2 = 0$$
$$R_B = \frac{20}{5} = 4 \text{ kN}$$

Vertical equilibrium:

$$R_A + R_B = 10 \quad \Rightarrow \quad R_A = 10 - 4 = 6 \text{ kN}$$

18. Shear Force & Bending Moment - Definitions Page 29

Shear Force (SF) at a section: Algebraic sum of all vertical forces to the LEFT or RIGHT of that section.

Sign Convention: Left UP = Positive SF. Right DOWN = Positive SF.

Bending Moment (BM) at a section: Algebraic sum of moments of all forces to the LEFT or RIGHT of that section about that section.

Sign Convention: Sagging (smile) = Positive BM. Hogging (frown) = Negative BM.

Important Relations:

$$\frac{dM}{dx} = V \quad \text{(Slope of BMD = SF)}$$
$$\frac{dV}{dx} = -w \quad \text{(Slope of SFD = -load intensity)}$$
Why: These relations help draw diagrams. If SF = 0, BM is max/min. If load is UDL, SF is linear, BM is parabolic.

Sign conventions for SF and BM

19. SFD & BMD - Point Loads Pages 30-33

Key Rules for Point Loads:

  1. SF diagram has sudden drop at point load. Drop = magnitude of load.
  2. Between loads, SF is constant (horizontal line).
  3. BM diagram has slope change at point load. Slope = SF.
  4. Max BM occurs where SF = 0 or changes sign.

Example: Central Point Load

Simply supported beam 4m long, point load 8 kN at center. Draw SFD & BMD.

Step 1: Reactions

$$R_A = R_B = \frac{8}{2} = 4 \text{ kN}$$

Step 2: SF Values

Just right of A: SF = +4 kN

Just left of center: SF = +4 kN

Just right of center: SF = 4 - 8 = -4 kN

Just left of B: SF = -4 kN

Step 3: BM Values

At A: BM = 0

At center: BM = 4 × 2 = 8 kN·m (max)

At B: BM = 0

Why: BM = Area under SF diagram. Triangle area = ½ × 2 × 4 = 4, but wait: BM = R_A × distance = 4 × 2 = 8 kN·m ✓

SFD is rectangular, BMD is triangular for point load

20. SFD & BMD - UDL Pages 34-36

Key Rules for UDL:

  1. SF diagram is sloping line. Slope = -w (load intensity).
  2. BM diagram is parabola. Max BM at center where SF = 0.
  3. Total load = w × L, acts at L/2 from each end.

For UDL over entire span L:

$$R_A = R_B = \frac{wL}{2}$$
$$SF_x = R_A - wx = \frac{wL}{2} - wx$$
$$BM_x = R_A x - \frac{wx^2}{2} = \frac{wLx}{2} - \frac{wx^2}{2}$$
$$BM_{max} = \frac{wL^2}{8} \quad \text{at } x = \frac{L}{2}$$
Why: SF starts at +wL/2, decreases linearly to -wL/2. BM is parabolic because integration of linear SF gives quadratic BM.

Example: UDL over Entire Span

Simply supported beam 6m, UDL 3 kN/m. Draw SFD & BMD.

Step 1: Reactions

$$R_A = R_B = \frac{3 \times 6}{2} = 9 \text{ kN}$$

Step 2: SF at Key Points

At A (x=0): SF = +9 kN

At center (x=3): SF = 9 - 3×3 = 0 kN

At B (x=6): SF = 9 - 3×6 = -9 kN

Step 3: BM at Key Points

At A: BM = 0

At center: BM = $\frac{3 \times 6^2}{8} = 13.5$ kN·m (max)

At B: BM = 0

SFD is sloping line, BMD is parabola for UDL

21. Overhanging Beams & Contraflexure Pages 37-40

Overhanging Beam: Simply supported beam extending beyond one or both supports.

Point of Contraflexure: Point where BM = 0 and changes sign. Also called point of inflection. Beam curvature reverses - from sagging to hogging.

Key Rules:

  1. Overhang creates negative BM at support.
  2. Contraflexure occurs where BM diagram crosses zero line.
  3. At contraflexure, beam has zero curvature - it’s straight at that point.

Example: Beam with One Overhang

Beam 6m between supports A & B, overhang 2m to right of B with point load 6 kN at free end. UDL 2 kN/m on AB. Find contraflexure point.

Step 1: Reactions

Taking moments about A: \(R_B \times 6 = 2 \times 6 \times 3 + 6 \times 8\) → \(R_B = 14\) kN

Vertical: \(R_A = 2 \times 6 + 6 - 14 = 4\) kN

Step 2: BM Equation for AB

$$M_x = 4x - \frac{2x^2}{2} = 4x - x^2$$

Step 3: Contraflexure where M = 0

$$4x - x^2 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad x(4-x) = 0$$
$$x = 0 \text{ (at A)} \text{ or } x = 4 \text{ m from A}$$
Why: BM is zero at A and at 4m from A. Between 0-4m beam sags, beyond 4m beam hogs due to overhang.

Overhang causes hogging BM and contraflexure point

22. Combined Loading - Multiple UDLs + Point Loads Pages 41-44

Method for Combined Loading:

  1. Calculate reactions using \(\sum M = 0\) and \(\sum F_y = 0\)
  2. Divide beam into sections at: supports, point loads, start/end of UDLs
  3. Find SF at each section boundary. SF changes suddenly at point loads, linearly over UDL
  4. Find BM at each section. BM slope = SF. Max BM where SF = 0
  5. Use superposition: Total effect = Sum of individual load effects
Why: Complex beams broken into simple segments. Each segment follows basic rules from sec19 & sec20.

Example: UDL + Point Load

Beam 8m: UDL 2 kN/m from A to 4m, point load 10 kN at 6m from A. Supports at A and B (8m). Draw SFD & BMD.

Step 1: Reactions

Total UDL = 2 × 4 = 8 kN at 2m from A

$$\sum M_A = 0: \quad R_B \times 8 = 8 \times 2 + 10 \times 6$$
$$R_B = \frac{16 + 60}{8} = 9.5 \text{ kN}$$
$$R_A = 8 + 10 - 9.5 = 8.5 \text{ kN}$$

Step 2: SF Diagram

At A: +8.5 kN → At 4m: 8.5 - 8 = +0.5 kN → At 6m: 0.5 - 10 = -9.5 kN → At B: -9.5 kN

Step 3: BM Max

SF = 0 at some point in UDL region: \(8.5 - 2x = 0\) → \(x = 4.25\) m

$$M_{max} = 8.5 \times 4.25 - 2 \times \frac{4.25^2}{2} = 18.063 \text{ kN·m}$$

23. Beams with Applied Couples/Moments Pages 45-48

Applied Couple/Moment: Pure rotation with no resultant force. Units: N·m or kN·m.

Key Rules:

  1. Couple causes NO change in SF diagram. SF remains same before and after.
  2. Couple causes SUDDEN JUMP in BM diagram. Jump = magnitude of couple.
  3. Clockwise couple = Positive jump in BM (increases BM).
  4. Anticlockwise couple = Negative jump in BM (decreases BM).
Why: Couple has zero resultant force, so SF unaffected. But it adds rotation, so BM jumps. Think of twisting the beam at that point.

Example: Clockwise Couple

Simply supported beam 6m, clockwise couple 12 kN·m at 2m from left. Draw BMD.

Step 1: Reactions

Couple has no vertical force, so \(R_A + R_B = 0\). Taking moments about A:

$$R_B \times 6 - 12 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad R_B = 2 \text{ kN up}$$
$$R_A = -2 \text{ kN (downward)}$$

Step 2: BM Values

Just left of couple: \(M = -2 \times 2 = -4\) kN·m

Just right of couple: \(M = -4 + 12 = +8\) kN·m (jump of +12)

At B: \(M = 0\)

Why: Clockwise couple adds positive BM. Without couple, BM would be -4 at 2m and -12 at 6m. Couple rotates beam up.

Clockwise couple causes positive jump in BMD

24. Inclined Loads Pages 49-51

Inclined Load: Load not vertical or horizontal, acts at angle θ to horizontal.

Resolution Method:

$$F_x = F \cos\theta \quad \text{(Horizontal component)}$$
$$F_y = F \sin\theta \quad \text{(Vertical component)}$$

Effects:

  1. Vertical component \(F_y\) causes SF and BM - use normal beam analysis.
  2. Horizontal component \(F_x\) causes axial force/thrust in beam - causes direct stress.
  3. For simply supported beams, \(F_x\) creates horizontal reactions at supports.
Why: Beam theory assumes loads perpendicular to beam axis. Inclined loads must be resolved. Horizontal part tries to push/pull beam axially.

Example: Inclined Point Load

Beam 5m, simply supported. 10 kN load at 3m from left, inclined 30° to vertical. Find reactions and max BM.

Step 1: Resolve Load

$$F_y = 10 \sin 30° = 10 \times 0.5 = 5 \text{ kN (vertical)}$$
$$F_x = 10 \cos 30° = 10 \times 0.866 = 8.66 \text{ kN (horizontal)}$$

Step 2: Vertical Reactions (for BM)

$$R_{By} \times 5 = 5 \times 3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad R_{By} = 3 \text{ kN}$$
$$R_{Ay} = 5 - 3 = 2 \text{ kN}$$

Step 3: Horizontal Reactions (for axial force)

Assume roller at B, pin at A: \(R_{Ax} = 8.66\) kN (to balance \(F_x\)), \(R_{Bx} = 0\)

Step 4: Max BM

$$M_{max} = R_{Ay} \times 3 = 2 \times 3 = 6 \text{ kN·m at load}$$
Why: Only vertical component used for BM. Horizontal component causes axial tension/compression of 8.66 kN.

Resolve inclined load into vertical and horizontal components

25. Reverse Analysis: Load from SFD Pages 52-53

Reverse Analysis: Given SFD, find loading on beam. Use differential relationships:

$$\frac{dV}{dx} = -w \quad \Rightarrow \quad w = -\frac{dV}{dx}$$
$$\frac{dM}{dx} = V$$

Rules to Identify Loads from SFD:

SFD FeatureLoading Type
Sudden vertical drop ↓Downward point load = magnitude of drop
Sudden vertical rise ↑Upward point load (reaction)
Constant slope downward ↘UDL. w = slope magnitude
Horizontal line →No load in that region
Zero SFMax or min BM at that section
Why: Derivative relationships. Load is rate of change of SF. SF is rate of change of BM. So you can integrate back up or differentiate down.

Example: Determine Loads from SFD

SFD shows: +6 kN at A, drops to +2 kN at 2m, then drops to -8 kN, then rises to 0 at B (6m). Find loads.

Step 1: Analyze SFD

At x=0: SF = +6 kN → Upward reaction \(R_A = 6\) kN

0 to 2m: SF constant at +6 → No load

At x=2m: Drop of 4 kN (from +6 to +2) → Downward point load 4 kN

2m to 4m: SF constant at +2 → No load

At x=4m: Drop of 10 kN (from +2 to -8) → Downward point load 10 kN

4m to 6m: SF constant at -8 → No load

At x=6m: Rise of 8 kN (from -8 to 0) → Upward reaction \(R_B = 8\) kN

Step 2: Verify

$$\sum F_y = 6 - 4 - 10 + 8 = 0 \, ✓$$

Answer: \(R_A = 6\) kN up, 4 kN down at 2m, 10 kN down at 4m, \(R_B = 8\) kN up
